Adopted February 2012, updated October 2023

RECOMMENDATION 1 — ASSESSING RISKS AND APPLYING A RISK-BASED APPROACH
================================================================================

[FATF-R1-001]
Countries must identify, assess, and understand their money laundering, terrorist financing, and proliferation financing risks. This national risk assessment (NRA) process must be documented and updated on a regular basis, with the findings used to allocate resources proportionally across sectors, geographic exposures, and customer types. The NRA must be made available to competent authorities, financial intelligence units, and obliged entities to the extent permitted by applicable law.

[FATF-R1-002]
Financial institutions and designated non-financial businesses and professions (DNFBPs) are required to identify, assess, and understand the money laundering and terrorist financing risks to which they are exposed. This institutional risk assessment (IRA) must be documented and kept up to date, taking into account risk factors including customers, countries or geographic areas, products, services, transactions, and delivery channels. The IRA must be approved by senior management and reviewed whenever there are material changes in the business profile.

[FATF-R1-003]
Under the risk-based approach, obliged entities must apply enhanced due diligence measures where higher risks are identified and may apply simplified due diligence measures where risks are demonstrably lower. The determination of "lower risk" must be supported by documented evidence and cannot be assumed; specific customer segments, jurisdictions, or product types must be assessed individually before simplified measures may be applied. Simplified due diligence does not exempt obliged entities from conducting basic customer due diligence; it merely permits a reduced intensity or frequency of ongoing monitoring.

[FATF-R1-004]
Where a country has identified higher risks, it shall ensure that its AML/CFT regime adequately addresses those risks, including by requiring financial institutions to take enhanced measures commensurate with the risk. Countries must establish mechanisms to ensure that financial institutions and DNFBPs understand the nature and level of their ML/TF risks and that internal policies, controls, and procedures are updated accordingly. National risk assessments should be conducted at intervals not exceeding four years, or more frequently upon material changes in the threat environment.

[FATF-R1-005]
The risk-based approach requires that allocation of resources within an obliged entity be proportional to the identified risk level. Compliance programs with uniform, standardized controls applied equally across all customers, products, and geographies without regard to risk differentiation do not satisfy the FATF standard for a risk-based approach. Competent authorities should assess whether an institution's risk assessment is genuine, comprehensive, and actually drives the intensity of due diligence applied.

[FATF-R1-006]
Financial institutions must document the risk factors considered in their institutional risk assessment, including the following categories: customer risk (e.g., PEPs, non-resident customers, legal persons and arrangements), geographic risk (e.g., countries subject to sanctions, jurisdictions with inadequate AML/CFT regimes), product and service risk (e.g., private banking, correspondent banking, wire transfers, virtual assets), and delivery channel risk (e.g., non-face-to-face business, reliance on intermediaries). The risk weightings applied to each factor must be documented and subject to periodic review by the compliance function.

[FATF-R1-007]
National risk assessments must consider the results of FATF mutual evaluation reports for relevant jurisdictions, findings from the FATF-style regional bodies (FSRBs), and assessments published by the Egmont Group and the Basel AML Index. Countries and institutions operating in high-risk jurisdictions or with significant cross-border exposures are expected to conduct enhanced country-level risk assessments and update their risk models accordingly when new FATF findings are published. Failure to integrate publicly available risk intelligence into an institution's IRA is considered a deficiency in the risk-based approach.

[FATF-R1-008]
The FATF Guidance on the Risk-Based Approach specifies that supervisors must assess whether an institution's risk assessment is proportionate to the nature, scale, and complexity of its business. Small institutions with simple business models may use streamlined risk assessments, but must still document the basis for any risk rating applied. Supervisors retain the authority to require an institution to revise its risk assessment if they determine that it fails to capture material risks or relies on unsupported assumptions about low-risk categorisations.

[FATF-R1-009]
Countries must ensure that the outcomes of risk assessments are shared with relevant stakeholders in a timely manner. Competent authorities, including financial intelligence units, supervisors, law enforcement, and prosecutorial bodies, must have access to the NRA findings and must use them to inform their own risk-based supervisory and investigative strategies. Financial institutions are encouraged to participate in public-private partnership forums where aggregated risk intelligence is shared, provided that information-sharing mechanisms comply with applicable privacy and confidentiality laws.

[FATF-R1-010]
An obliged entity's risk-based approach must be embedded in its governance framework and reflected in board-approved policies and procedures. The board of directors bears ultimate responsibility for ensuring that the institution's AML/CFT risk appetite is explicitly defined and that the compliance program is adequately resourced to manage identified risks within that appetite. Material changes to the institution's risk profile — including entry into new markets, launch of new products, or onboarding of new customer segments — must trigger a reassessment of the IRA before or concurrent with the change.

RECOMMENDATION 6 — TARGETED FINANCIAL SANCTIONS RELATED TO TERRORISM AND TERRORIST FINANCING
================================================================================

[FATF-R6-001]
Countries must implement targeted financial sanctions (TFS) without delay in relation to persons and entities designated under United Nations Security Council Resolutions 1267 (1999), 1989 (2011), and 2253 (2015) concerning Al-Qaida, and Resolution 1373 (2001) concerning terrorism and terrorist financing. "Without delay" is interpreted by FATF as meaning within hours of a designation, not merely within the business day. Financial institutions must have systems capable of screening customer bases and transaction parties against consolidated lists immediately upon publication of any new or amended designation.

[FATF-R6-002]
Financial institutions must freeze without delay and without prior notice to the customer all funds, financial assets, and economic resources owned, held, or controlled — directly or indirectly — by designated persons or entities. Freezing obligations apply not only to accounts and deposits but to all forms of property, including real estate interests, securities, derivatives positions, insurance policy cash values, and any other tangible or intangible assets held at or through the institution. The institution must not permit any debit, transfer, withdrawal, or other disposition of frozen assets unless a licence, exception, or derogation has been granted by the competent authority.

[FATF-R6-003]
The screening obligation under Recommendation 6 extends beyond existing customers to cover all prospective customers at onboarding, all transaction counterparties in payment and trade finance operations, and all persons acting on behalf of customers, including authorised signatories, directors, beneficial owners, and attorneys-in-fact. Institutions must screen against the UNSC consolidated list, applicable domestic designated persons lists, and any regional lists (e.g., EU Council Regulations) that apply by virtue of the institution's jurisdictional presence. Screening must be performed using name-matching algorithms capable of identifying spelling variations, transliterations, aliases, and name-order variations.

[FATF-R6-004]
Where a financial institution identifies a potential match against a TFS list, it must immediately freeze the relevant assets or refuse the transaction pending verification and must report the match to the competent authority and the financial intelligence unit as required by national law. The institution must not "tip off" the customer or any third party that a potential match has been identified or that a report has been filed. Institutions are required to maintain records of all potential matches investigated, including the outcome of the investigation and the basis for clearing or confirming the match.

[FATF-R6-005]
Countries must establish a mechanism by which persons or entities that have been erroneously designated, or whose assets have been frozen in error, may petition the competent authority for unfreezing or delisting. Institutions must cooperate with the competent authority in such proceedings and must promptly unfreeze assets upon receipt of a lawful unfreezing order. Pending a determination on a delisting petition, institutions must maintain the freeze on relevant assets and may only release funds for basic expenses as explicitly authorised by applicable UNSC resolutions or domestic implementing legislation.

[FATF-R6-006]
Financial institutions must establish and maintain documented procedures for responding to TFS hits, including escalation protocols, decision trees for confirmed versus potential matches, licensing procedures for humanitarian exceptions, and timelines for reporting to competent authorities. These procedures must be tested through periodic simulation exercises and updated whenever there are material changes to the applicable sanctions framework. Senior management must receive regular reporting on TFS screening performance, including system coverage rates, hit volumes, false positive ratios, and investigation turnaround times.

[FATF-R6-007]
The obligation to screen under Recommendation 6 is not limited to real-time transaction screening; it extends to periodic retrospective screening of the entire customer base whenever a new designation is published. Institutions must have technical capabilities to complete a full population screening within a timeframe commensurate with the risk — generally within 24 hours for high-priority UNSC additions. Institutions that rely on batch-processing screening systems with update intervals exceeding 24 hours must document the residual risk and implement compensating controls, such as transaction-level screening, to mitigate the gap.

[FATF-R6-008]
Countries must ensure that TFS obligations apply not only to banks but to all financial institutions and DNFBPs within the FATF definition, including money services businesses, securities dealers, insurance companies, virtual asset service providers (VASPs), and trust and company service providers. Non-bank financial institutions that process payments or hold client assets on behalf of third parties bear the same TFS screening obligations as banks and are subject to supervision and enforcement by the relevant competent authority. Countries must ensure that the TFS regime is not circumvented through the use of non-bank intermediaries.

RECOMMENDATION 7 — TARGETED FINANCIAL SANCTIONS RELATED TO PROLIFERATION
================================================================================

[FATF-R7-001]
Countries must implement targeted financial sanctions without delay to comply with UN Security Council Resolutions concerning the prevention, suppression, and disruption of proliferation financing of weapons of mass destruction. The primary resolutions establishing these obligations are UNSCR 1718 (2006) and its successor resolutions relating to the Democratic People's Republic of Korea (DPRK), and UNSCR 2231 (2015) relating to the Islamic Republic of Iran. Financial institutions must maintain current, up-to-date screening lists for both DPRK and Iran designations and must apply TFS measures to any persons, entities, vessels, or aircraft appearing on those lists.

[FATF-R7-002]
Proliferation financing refers to the act of providing funds or financial services for the purpose of, or knowing they will be used for, the manufacture, acquisition, stockpiling, development, export, trans-shipment, brokering, transport, transfer, stockpiling, or use of nuclear, chemical, biological, or radiological weapons. Financial institutions dealing in trade finance, export credit, shipping finance, or cross-border payments must apply heightened scrutiny to transactions involving dual-use goods, technology transfers, or counterparties located in or connected to DPRK or Iran. Institutions must be particularly alert to indicators of sanctions evasion, including the use of front companies, layering through multiple jurisdictions, and the falsification of trade documentation.

[FATF-R7-003]
The UNSC DPRK sanctions regime established under UNSCR 1718 (2006) and subsequent resolutions, including UNSCR 2270 (2016), 2321 (2016), 2371 (2017), 2375 (2017), and 2397 (2017), imposes an obligation on all UN Member States to freeze the funds, other financial assets, and economic resources of designated individuals and entities. Financial institutions must ensure that their sanctions screening systems include the DPRK Consolidated List maintained by the UN Security Council Committee established pursuant to UNSCR 1718. Institutions must also screen against the OFAC North Korea Sanctions Regulations list (31 C.F.R. Part 510) and equivalent domestic implementing lists.

[FATF-R7-004]
The Proliferation Financing Risk Assessment (PFRA) is a distinct obligation from the general ML/TF risk assessment and must be conducted separately. FATF guidance published in June 2021 clarifies that the PFRA should assess the exposure of the institution to the risk that its products, services, and customer relationships might be used to finance proliferation activities. The PFRA must consider the institution's geographic reach, the nature of its correspondent banking relationships, its trade finance activities, and the extent to which it processes transactions involving dual-use goods or sanctioned jurisdictions.

[FATF-R7-005]
Financial institutions must implement targeted financial sanctions related to proliferation across all business lines, not merely those with an obvious trade finance or export component. Wire transfers, foreign exchange transactions, trade finance instruments, securities transactions, and insurance products are all potential vectors for proliferation financing. Institutions must apply specific red flag indicators for proliferation financing, including unusual routing through multiple jurisdictions, involvement of shipping companies or freight forwarders without an identifiable nexus to the stated trade, payments to or from jurisdictions with known proliferation finance risks, and inconsistencies between declared goods and actual transaction values.

[FATF-R7-006]
Countries must ensure that competent authorities have adequate tools and powers to investigate and prosecute proliferation financing offences, and that the financial intelligence unit receives and is able to disseminate financial intelligence relevant to proliferation financing. Financial institutions must file suspicious transaction reports where they suspect or have reasonable grounds to suspect that a transaction or attempted transaction is connected to proliferation financing. The STR filing obligation for proliferation financing is separate from and in addition to the general ML/TF STR obligation, and institutions must ensure that their transaction monitoring systems include specific typologies and red flags for proliferation activity.

RECOMMENDATION 10 — CUSTOMER DUE DILIGENCE
================================================================================

[FATF-R10-001]
Financial institutions must apply customer due diligence (CDD) measures when: establishing business relationships; carrying out occasional transactions above USD/EUR 15,000 (whether carried out in a single transaction or several linked transactions); there is a suspicion of money laundering or terrorist financing regardless of transaction value; and when there are doubts about the veracity or adequacy of previously obtained customer identification data. The USD/EUR 15,000 threshold is a minimum; institutions in higher-risk sectors or jurisdictions may be required by competent authorities to apply lower thresholds.

[FATF-R10-002]
CDD measures comprise four core elements: (i) identifying the customer and verifying their identity using reliable, independent source documents, data, or information; (ii) identifying the beneficial owner and taking reasonable measures to verify their identity, including understanding the ownership and control structure of legal persons and arrangements; (iii) understanding and, where appropriate, obtaining information on the purpose and intended nature of the business relationship; and (iv) conducting ongoing due diligence on the business relationship, including scrutiny of transactions to ensure they are consistent with the institution's knowledge of the customer. All four elements must be applied; the omission of any element constitutes a CDD failure.

[FATF-R10-003]
Beneficial ownership identification requires financial institutions to identify the natural person(s) who ultimately own or control the customer, and to take reasonable measures to verify the identity of those persons. The FATF standard defines a beneficial owner as the natural person who ultimately owns or controls a customer and/or the natural person on whose behalf a transaction is being conducted. For legal persons, institutions must identify and verify the identity of the natural person(s) holding a controlling ownership interest, or where no natural person can be identified through the ownership chain, the natural person exercising control through other means, or where no such person is identifiable, the natural person in the position of senior managing official.

[FATF-R10-004]
The threshold for identifying beneficial ownership in legal persons is typically set at 25% ownership or control; however, FATF guidance makes clear that this is a maximum threshold and that institutions operating under a risk-based approach should apply lower thresholds where the risk profile of the customer or the jurisdiction warrants it. Where a customer's ownership structure involves multiple layers of legal entities, trusts, or nominee arrangements, the institution must "look through" each layer until it has identified the ultimate natural person beneficial owner(s). Institutions are not permitted to treat the identification of a corporate shareholder as satisfying the beneficial ownership requirement; the chain must be followed to its natural person endpoint.

[FATF-R10-005]
Ongoing due diligence requires financial institutions to scrutinise transactions throughout the business relationship to ensure they are consistent with the institution's knowledge of the customer, their business, and risk profile, including the source of funds. Ongoing monitoring must be commensurate with the risk rating of the customer; high-risk customers must be subject to enhanced ongoing monitoring with more frequent review cycles, lower transaction alert thresholds, and more intensive investigation of unusual activity. The interval between periodic CDD reviews must be defined in policy and must reflect the customer's risk classification: typically annually for high-risk, every two to three years for medium-risk, and every three to five years for lower-risk customers.

[FATF-R10-006]
Financial institutions must understand the source of funds used in the business relationship and, for higher-risk customers, the source of wealth of the beneficial owner. "Source of funds" refers to the origin of the specific funds used in the transaction or account; "source of wealth" refers to the activities that have generated the total net worth of the customer or beneficial owner. Both concepts are distinct and must be addressed separately; documentation obtained to establish source of funds does not satisfy the source of wealth requirement. For PEPs, trusts, and high-net-worth customers, institutions must obtain documentary evidence to corroborate claimed sources of wealth.

[FATF-R10-007]
Where a financial institution is unable to satisfy itself regarding the identity of the customer, the beneficial owner, or the purpose of the business relationship, it must not open an account, commence the business relationship, or perform the transaction, and should consider filing a suspicious transaction report with the financial intelligence unit. For existing customers where CDD refresh reveals material gaps or inconsistencies that cannot be resolved, the institution must consider restricting or terminating the business relationship. A decision to terminate a relationship on AML/CFT grounds must be documented, retained, and reported to senior management; the tipping-off prohibition applies to such decisions.

[FATF-R10-008]
The timing of CDD verification is subject to the risk-based approach; while verification must generally be completed before the establishment of the business relationship or the execution of the transaction, institutions may complete verification during the establishment of the business relationship where the risk of ML/TF is low and it is necessary not to interrupt the normal conduct of business. In such cases, institutions must apply risk management procedures, including limitations on the volume or value of transactions that may be executed before verification is complete and mandatory follow-up procedures with defined timeframes. Failure to complete CDD within the stipulated follow-up period must result in termination of the relationship and, if suspicion has arisen, a suspicious transaction report.

[FATF-R10-009]
Simplified customer due diligence may be applied in lower-risk situations but must not result in the complete exemption of a customer from all CDD requirements. Even in simplified CDD scenarios, institutions must collect sufficient information to understand the nature of the customer relationship and must conduct some form of ongoing monitoring. Simplified CDD is not appropriate where there are indications of higher risk or where the customer, beneficial owner, or transaction exhibits red flags that suggest ML/TF risk, regardless of the customer's prima facie categorisation.

[FATF-R10-010]
Third-party reliance for CDD allows financial institutions to rely on the due diligence performed by another financial institution or DNFBP that is subject to AML/CFT requirements. However, the institution relying on the third party must: (i) immediately obtain from the third party the required CDD information; (ii) satisfy itself that the third party is regulated and supervised for AML/CFT purposes; and (iii) ensure that the third party is able to provide, upon request, copies of the identification and verification data and other relevant documentation within a specified timeframe, typically five business days. The relying institution retains ultimate responsibility for compliance with the CDD requirements and cannot transfer liability to the third party.

RECOMMENDATION 12 — POLITICALLY EXPOSED PERSONS
================================================================================

[FATF-R12-001]
Financial institutions must have risk management systems in place to determine whether a customer or beneficial owner is a politically exposed person (PEP). A PEP is defined as an individual who is or has been entrusted with a prominent public function, whether domestically or by a foreign government or international organisation. Prominent public functions include heads of state or government, senior politicians, senior government officials, judicial or military officials, senior executives of state-owned enterprises, and important political party officials. Family members and close associates of PEPs must also be identified and subject to the same enhanced due diligence measures.

[FATF-R12-002]
Enhanced due diligence (EDD) for foreign PEPs is mandatory and must be applied regardless of the risk assessment of the individual customer's profile. EDD for PEPs requires institutions to: (i) obtain senior management approval before establishing or continuing a business relationship with a PEP; (ii) take reasonable measures to establish the source of wealth and source of funds of the PEP and their immediate family members; and (iii) conduct enhanced ongoing monitoring of the business relationship. "Senior management approval" must involve a named individual of sufficient seniority to exercise genuine oversight; approval by a junior compliance officer does not satisfy this requirement.

[FATF-R12-003]
Source of wealth documentation for PEPs must be proportionate to the risk presented and must go beyond mere declarations by the customer. Acceptable forms of source of wealth evidence include: official salary confirmation from the relevant government authority, independently verified financial statements, documented evidence of legitimate business interests, inheritance documentation corroborated by public records, real estate transaction records, and investment portfolio statements from regulated firms. Where the declared source of wealth is inconsistent with the known remuneration of the public function held, or where public records suggest wealth inconsistent with legitimate income, the institution must treat this as a red flag and conduct enhanced scrutiny or decline the relationship.

[FATF-R12-004]
Domestic PEPs and persons entrusted with prominent functions by international organisations must be subject to the risk-based approach; unlike foreign PEPs, enhanced due diligence is not automatically mandatory but must be applied when the risk-based assessment indicates higher risk. However, in practice, FATF guidance and most supervisory frameworks in major financial centres require EDD for all PEPs regardless of nationality, given the inherent corruption risk associated with the holding of prominent public functions. Institutions must document the basis for their risk classification of domestic PEPs and the specific EDD measures applied.

[FATF-R12-005]
The PEP status of a customer or beneficial owner must be monitored on an ongoing basis throughout the business relationship. Individuals who are appointed to prominent public positions after the initial CDD review must be identified and subjected to PEP EDD procedures without undue delay. Where a customer's PEP status arises from a family member's appointment to a prominent position, the institution must reassess the customer's risk profile and apply appropriate EDD measures. Institutions must subscribe to commercially available PEP databases and must ensure that these databases are updated at sufficient frequency to capture new appointments.

[FATF-R12-006]
Former PEPs — individuals who have relinquished their prominent public function — remain subject to the risk-based approach. FATF guidance recognises that the risks associated with PEP status do not automatically disappear upon departure from public office, and that institutions must apply a cooling-off period during which enhanced due diligence continues to be applied. The appropriate duration of the cooling-off period depends on the risk assessment of the individual and their jurisdiction; in high-corruption-risk jurisdictions or where the individual held positions with significant influence over public funds, the cooling-off period may extend for five years or longer.

[FATF-R12-007]
Family members and close associates of PEPs are subject to the same EDD requirements as the PEP themselves. "Family members" includes spouses or civil partners, children and their spouses or partners, and parents. "Close associates" includes natural persons who are known to be joint beneficial owners of legal entities or arrangements with the PEP, or who are known to have close business relations with the PEP, or who are sole beneficial owners of a legal entity or arrangement set up for the de facto benefit of the PEP. Institutions must apply reasonable measures to identify family members and close associates at onboarding and must document the steps taken to identify such persons.

[FATF-R12-008]
Institutions must apply enhanced transaction monitoring to PEP accounts, including lower threshold alerts, increased scrutiny of large or unusual cash transactions, and review of transactions inconsistent with the PEP's stated source of wealth or income. Transactions involving jurisdictions known for high levels of public corruption must attract heightened scrutiny when linked to PEP accounts. The Corruption Perceptions Index (CPI) published annually by Transparency International is a commonly used reference for identifying higher-corruption-risk jurisdictions; institutions may use the CPI as one factor in their PEP risk scoring but must not rely on it as the sole determinant.

RECOMMENDATION 15 — NEW TECHNOLOGIES
================================================================================

[FATF-R15-001]
Countries and financial institutions must assess and manage the ML/TF risks associated with new technologies, new products and business practices, and new or developing delivery mechanisms for existing products or services. This obligation requires a forward-looking risk assessment that anticipates the emergence of new risk vectors rather than merely reacting to identified abuses. Institutions must ensure that their AML/CFT frameworks are updated to address new technology risks as part of the annual review cycle, and must not deploy new products or services using new technologies without first completing an ML/TF risk assessment specific to that technology.

[FATF-R15-002]
Virtual assets and virtual asset service providers (VASPs) are explicitly addressed under Recommendation 15 as amended in June 2019. Countries must ensure that VASPs are regulated for AML/CFT purposes, licensed or registered, and subject to effective systems of monitoring and supervision. VASPs must apply CDD measures, record-keeping requirements, and STR filing obligations consistent with the FATF Recommendations applicable to financial institutions. Financial institutions that interact with VASPs — through correspondent relationships, fiat on-ramps, or other means — must conduct enhanced due diligence on their VASP counterparts and satisfy themselves that the VASP's AML/CFT controls are adequate.

[FATF-R15-003]
The Travel Rule, as applied to virtual assets, requires VASPs and financial institutions to obtain, hold, and transmit required originator and beneficiary information when conducting virtual asset transfers. For virtual asset transfers of USD/EUR 1,000 or above, originator information must include: the originator's name, the originator's account number or equivalent identifier (e.g., virtual asset wallet address), and the originator's address, or national identity number, or customer identification number, or date and place of birth. Beneficiary information must include the beneficiary's name and account number or wallet address. VASPs must have technical systems capable of transmitting this information securely to the beneficiary VASP.

[FATF-R15-004]
Financial institutions must assess the ML/TF risks posed by the use of artificial intelligence, machine learning, and automated decision-making systems in their own AML/CFT compliance processes. While AI and ML tools may enhance the effectiveness of transaction monitoring and customer risk scoring, their use introduces model risk, explainability risk, and the risk of systematic bias in risk assessment. Institutions must ensure that AI-driven AML tools are subject to model validation, regular back-testing, and human oversight, and that the basis for automated risk classifications can be explained to regulators and to customers where required by law.

[FATF-R15-005]
Decentralised finance (DeFi) protocols present specific AML/CFT risks arising from the absence of a centralised intermediary that can be identified as an obliged entity. Countries must assess whether and to what extent DeFi protocols and their associated governance token holders or developers fall within the VASP definition and are therefore subject to AML/CFT regulation. Financial institutions offering access to DeFi products through custody, staking, or investment management services must ensure that their own CDD and transaction monitoring obligations are applied to customer activity involving DeFi protocols, regardless of the decentralised nature of the underlying technology.

[FATF-R15-006]
Fintech companies operating as payment service providers, e-money institutions, or embedded finance platforms must conduct comprehensive ML/TF risk assessments covering the specific risks associated with their technology-driven delivery channels, including mobile applications, API-based integrations with third-party platforms, and automated account opening processes using digital identity verification. The use of non-face-to-face onboarding channels does not exempt fintech institutions from the obligation to verify customer identity to the standard required under Recommendation 10; institutions must use equivalent alternative verification methods, such as electronic identity verification against trusted government databases, video-based verification, or certified digital identity solutions.

RECOMMENDATION 16 — WIRE TRANSFERS (TRAVEL RULE)
================================================================================

[FATF-R16-001]
The Travel Rule requires that financial institutions ensure that required originator and beneficiary information accompanies wire transfers throughout the payment chain. For wire transfers of USD/EUR 1,000 or above (domestic threshold may vary by jurisdiction), the ordering financial institution must obtain and transmit the following originator information: the originator's full name; the originator's account number (IBAN or equivalent); and the originator's address, national identity number, customer identification number, or date and place of birth. Incomplete or missing originator information must cause the intermediary or beneficiary institution to apply enhanced scrutiny or reject the transfer.

[FATF-R16-002]
Beneficiary institution obligations under the Travel Rule include: verifying that originator information is present on inbound wire transfers; applying enhanced scrutiny to transfers with missing or incomplete originator information; and implementing risk-based procedures for determining whether to execute, reject, or hold a transfer pending receipt of complete information. Beneficiary institutions must not automatically return funds without reporting to the financial intelligence unit where the absence of originator information raises a suspicion of ML/TF. The tipping-off prohibition applies to any communications with the ordering institution that might alert the originator that a report has been or is being filed.

[FATF-R16-003]
Intermediary financial institutions processing wire transfers as pass-through participants in the payment chain must ensure that all originator and beneficiary information that accompanies the transfer is retained and transmitted to the next institution in the chain. Where the messaging format used does not have the technical capacity to carry all required information, the intermediary institution must establish a mechanism — which may include sending information via a parallel communication channel — to ensure that the receiving institution has access to the required data. Intermediary institutions that strip, truncate, or fail to pass on required Travel Rule data are in breach of the FATF standard regardless of technical limitations in the payment messaging system.

[FATF-R16-004]
For domestic wire transfers, a simplified set of information may be transmitted with the transfer, provided that the full originator information can be made available to the beneficiary institution and competent authorities within three business days of receiving a request. The ordering institution must maintain full CDD records for the originator and must have systems in place to retrieve and transmit this information promptly upon request. For international wire transfers, no such simplification applies; the full set of originator and beneficiary information must accompany the transfer at the time of execution.

[FATF-R16-005]
Batch transfers or payroll files processed through automated clearing house (ACH) or equivalent batch payment systems must comply with the Travel Rule in a manner appropriate to the batch format; the originator information for each underlying transaction must be available and retraceable. Institutions must not use the batch format as a means of obscuring the identity of individual originators or beneficiaries. Where a batch file is submitted by a corporate customer for multiple underlying payees, the institution must satisfy itself regarding the identity and legitimacy of the underlying transactions and must apply appropriate transaction monitoring to identify anomalous patterns within the batch.

[FATF-R16-006]
Financial institutions must establish policies and procedures for handling wire transfers with missing, incomplete, or inconsistent originator or beneficiary information. These procedures must define: the threshold below which transfers are automatically rejected versus held for investigation; the escalation path for transfers with suspicious characteristics; the timeframe within which a determination must be made; and the reporting obligations where ML/TF suspicion arises. Records of all such determinations — including the outcome, the basis for the decision, and any reports filed — must be retained for the period specified by the applicable record-keeping requirements.

[FATF-R16-007]
The Travel Rule is distinct from and complementary to the sanctions screening obligation; compliance with one does not constitute compliance with the other. Institutions must apply both the Travel Rule information transmission and retention requirements and the sanctions screening requirement to every wire transfer. Where Travel Rule information reveals the identity of an originator or beneficiary who is a designated person, the sanctions screening obligation requires immediate freezing of assets and reporting, while the Travel Rule record-keeping obligation requires that the complete transfer information be retained and made available to competent authorities.

RECOMMENDATION 20 — REPORTING OF SUSPICIOUS TRANSACTIONS
================================================================================

[FATF-R20-001]
Financial institutions and DNFBPs must promptly report to the financial intelligence unit (FIU) their suspicions that funds are the proceeds of a criminal activity or are related to terrorist financing, regardless of the amount of the transaction. The obligation to report is triggered by suspicion, not by certainty; institutions are not required to have evidence of a predicate offence before filing a suspicious transaction report (STR). A reasonable suspicion standard applies, meaning that there must be an objective basis for the suspicion that a trained compliance officer would recognise as indicative of potential ML/TF activity.

[FATF-R20-002]
The tipping-off prohibition is a core element of the STR regime: financial institutions and their directors, officers, and employees are prohibited from disclosing to the customer or any third party that an STR has been filed or is contemplated, or that an investigation is being conducted. Violation of the tipping-off prohibition may result in criminal sanctions, regulatory penalties, and reputational consequences. Institutions must train all relevant staff on the tipping-off prohibition and must have internal escalation procedures that prevent inadvertent disclosure; account managers, relationship bankers, and customer service staff must be specifically instructed not to alert customers to AML investigations.

[FATF-R20-003]
The STR must be filed promptly upon the formation of suspicion, which in most jurisdictions means within one to three business days of the determination that a report is required, although some jurisdictions impose shorter timeframes for certain categories of transactions or designated persons. The STR must be filed with the national FIU regardless of whether the transaction has already been completed or was interrupted; attempts at suspicious transactions are reportable in most jurisdictions. Institutions must ensure that their STR filing systems capture the full narrative detail required by the FIU, including the specific facts, transactions, and accounts that form the basis of the suspicion.

[FATF-R20-004]
Internal suspicious activity reports (SARs) submitted by front-line staff to the compliance function must be reviewed and adjudicated within a defined timeframe to ensure timely external reporting. Institutions must not permit internal SARs to remain unreviewed for periods that would result in a delay in external reporting to the FIU. The compliance officer or Money Laundering Reporting Officer (MLRO) is responsible for reviewing all internal SARs and making the determination whether to file an external STR; this determination must be documented and records retained, including in cases where the MLRO decides not to file an external STR.

[FATF-R20-005]
Safe harbour protections available to institutions and their employees for STR filings in good faith must be clearly communicated to all staff involved in AML/CFT compliance. The good faith standard requires that the reporter genuinely believed at the time of filing that there were reasonable grounds to suspect ML/TF activity; it does not require that the suspicion ultimately prove correct. Institutions should adopt a "file if in doubt" policy to encourage appropriate use of safe harbour protections and to avoid under-reporting, which is itself a compliance failure that may attract supervisory sanctions.

[FATF-R20-006]
Post-STR account management requires institutions to balance the obligation not to tip off against the need to continue providing services. In many cases, institutions may continue to maintain the customer relationship and provide services after filing an STR, subject to enhanced monitoring and with appropriate senior management awareness. Institutions must not automatically exit a customer relationship upon filing an STR; the decision to exit must be based on a risk assessment that considers the nature of the suspicion, the customer's response to any permissible enquiries, and whether continued servicing would pose unacceptable risk.

[FATF-R20-007]
Institutions must conduct quality assurance reviews of their STR program to assess the completeness, timeliness, and quality of filings. Quality assurance metrics should include: the ratio of internal SARs to external STRs filed (a high no-file rate may indicate under-reporting); the average time from detection to external filing; the proportion of STRs that contain adequate narrative detail for FIU analysis; and feedback received from the FIU on the usefulness of filings. The results of STR quality assurance reviews must be reported to senior management and the board compliance committee and used to improve detection and reporting processes.

[FATF-R20-008]
Financial institutions must ensure that transaction monitoring systems are calibrated to generate alerts that lead to genuine STR filings, not merely to produce high volumes of low-quality alerts that overwhelm the compliance function. Excessive false positive rates in transaction monitoring undermine the effectiveness of the STR program by diverting compliance resources from genuine suspicious activity. Institutions must conduct regular tuning exercises on their transaction monitoring models, using feedback from investigations and STR outcomes, and must document the rationale for any changes to alert thresholds or scenario parameters.

KEY DEFINITIONS — FATF GLOSSARY
================================================================================

[FATF-DEF-001]
"Money Laundering" as used in the FATF Recommendations refers to the processing of criminal proceeds to disguise their illegal origin. The three classical stages of money laundering are: placement (introduction of illicit funds into the financial system), layering (concealment of the trail of the proceeds through a series of financial transactions), and integration (reinvestment of the now-laundered proceeds into the legitimate economy). In practice, these stages often overlap or occur simultaneously, and institutions must be alert to indicators of ML activity at each stage. The predicate offences from which ML proceeds can derive are defined broadly in FATF Recommendation 3.

[FATF-DEF-002]
"Terrorist Financing" refers to the financing of terrorist acts, terrorist organisations, and individual terrorists. Critically, the source of funds used for terrorist financing may be legitimate (e.g., donations, business revenue) or illicit, making terrorist financing more difficult to detect than money laundering. The value of transactions associated with terrorist financing may be small and may not conform to the transaction patterns typically associated with commercial money laundering. Institutions must ensure that their transaction monitoring models include specific typologies for terrorist financing, including small-value transfers to high-risk jurisdictions, crowdfunding activity with indicators of extremist connections, and the use of virtual assets for terrorist financing.

[FATF-DEF-003]
"Beneficial Owner" means the natural person(s) who ultimately own or control a customer, and/or the natural person on whose behalf a transaction is being conducted. This includes persons who exercise ultimate effective control over a legal person or arrangement. For the purposes of wire transfer compliance under Recommendation 16, the beneficial owner of the originating account must be identified and verified as part of the standard CDD process; the originator information transmitted with the wire transfer relates to the account holder, which may differ from the ultimate beneficial owner where the account is held in the name of a legal entity.

[FATF-DEF-004]
"Financial Institution" for the purposes of the FATF Recommendations means any natural or legal person who conducts as a business one or more of the following activities: acceptance of deposits and other repayable funds; lending; financial leasing; money or value transfer services; issuing and managing means of payment; financial guarantees and commitments; trading in money market instruments, foreign exchange, exchange, interest rate, or index instruments, transferable securities, and commodity futures; participation in securities issues and provision of financial services related to such issues; individual and collective portfolio management; safekeeping and administration of cash or liquid securities on behalf of other persons; and insurance products.

[FATF-DEF-005]
"Virtual Asset Service Provider" (VASP) means any natural or legal person who is not covered elsewhere under the FATF Recommendations, and as a business conducts one or more of the following activities or operations for or on behalf of another natural or legal person: exchange between virtual assets and fiat currencies; exchange between one or more forms of virtual assets; transfer of virtual assets; safekeeping and/or administration of virtual assets or instruments enabling control over virtual assets; and participation in and provision of financial services related to an issuer's offer and/or sale of a virtual asset. Countries must apply AML/CFT requirements to VASPs in a manner consistent with those applied to financial institutions.

[FATF-DEF-006]
"Correspondent Banking" refers to the provision of banking services by one bank (the "correspondent") to another bank (the "respondent"), enabling the respondent to provide cross-border payment services to its customers without the need for the respondent to establish a physical presence in the correspondent's jurisdiction. Correspondent banking relationships create a significant AML/CFT risk because the correspondent relies on the respondent's CDD processes for the customers whose transactions the correspondent processes. Financial institutions providing correspondent banking services must apply enhanced due diligence to respondent institutions and must satisfy themselves that the respondent has adequate AML/CFT controls before establishing or continuing the relationship.

[FATF-DEF-007]
"Shell Bank" means a bank that has no physical presence in the country in which it is incorporated and licensed, and which is not affiliated with a regulated financial group that is subject to effective consolidated supervision. Financial institutions are prohibited from establishing or maintaining correspondent banking relationships with shell banks. For the purposes of this definition, a "physical presence" means a meaningful mind and management presence in the jurisdiction, including a local management team with genuine decision-making authority; the mere existence of an agent or a registered address does not constitute physical presence.

[FATF-DEF-008]
"Designated Non-Financial Businesses and Professions" (DNFBPs) include, for the purposes of the FATF Recommendations: casinos, real estate agents, dealers in precious metals and stones, lawyers, notaries, other independent legal professionals, and accountants when they prepare or carry out transactions for clients concerning buying and selling of real estate, management of client money/securities, management of bank/savings/securities accounts, organisation of company creation/operation/management, and creation/operation/management of companies and trusts. Trust and company service providers are also included in the DNFBP category.

[FATF-DEF-009]
"Suspicious Transaction Report" (STR) is a report filed by a financial institution or DNFBP with the financial intelligence unit where the institution suspects or has reasonable grounds to suspect that funds are the proceeds of a criminal activity or are related to terrorist financing, or that the customer is attempting to conceal or disguise such activity. The STR is the primary mechanism by which the private sector contributes financial intelligence to law enforcement and the FIU. The quality of STR reporting — including the specificity of the reported facts, the clarity of the narrative, and the accuracy of the account and customer information provided — directly affects the utility of the report to downstream law enforcement action.

[FATF-DEF-010]
"Financial Intelligence Unit" (FIU) is a central, national agency responsible for receiving, analysing, and disseminating to the competent authorities financial intelligence — including suspicious transaction reports — for the purpose of combating money laundering, associated predicate offences, and terrorist financing. The Egmont Group of Financial Intelligence Units is the international forum for cooperation among FIUs and has established principles of information exchange that govern the sharing of financial intelligence between member FIUs. National FIUs must have access to financial and other information from a broad range of public and private sources and must be able to disseminate intelligence to law enforcement and other competent authorities in a timely manner.

ADDITIONAL PROVISIONS — RECORD KEEPING AND INTERNATIONAL COOPERATION
================================================================================

[FATF-R11-001]
Financial institutions must maintain records of all customer identification and verification data, account files, business correspondence, and transaction records for a minimum of five years following the termination of the business relationship or the execution of the occasional transaction. Record-keeping requirements apply equally to digital and paper records; institutions that maintain records in digital format must ensure that the records are stored securely, are protected against alteration, and remain retrievable throughout the retention period. Institutions must ensure that records can be provided promptly to competent authorities upon request; delays in producing required records are treated as a serious compliance failure.

[FATF-R11-002]
Transaction records retained under the five-year record-keeping requirement must contain sufficient detail to reconstruct individual transactions, including the nature and date of the transaction, the type and amount of currency involved, the type and identifying number of any account used, and the destination of the funds. For wire transfers, records must include all originator and beneficiary information transmitted with the payment, as well as any intermediary institution information where available. Institutions that purge transaction records before the expiry of the retention period — even for technical reasons such as system migrations — commit a record-keeping violation that may result in supervisory enforcement action.

[FATF-R37-001]
Countries must provide the widest possible range of mutual legal assistance to facilitate the investigation, prosecution, and related proceedings in relation to money laundering and terrorist financing. The obligation to provide mutual legal assistance (MLA) applies regardless of whether the offence is considered to involve fiscal matters by the requested country. Countries must not refuse MLA on the grounds of bank secrecy, and must have mechanisms to identify, assess, and respond to MLA requests expeditiously.

[FATF-R38-001]
Countries must have the authority to take expeditious action in response to requests by foreign countries to identify, freeze, seize, and confiscate property laundered, proceeds from money laundering or predicate offences, instrumentalities used in or intended for use in these offences, or property of corresponding value. Financial institutions must cooperate with competent authorities in executing asset freezing and confiscation orders, including orders issued pursuant to MLA requests from foreign jurisdictions. Institutions must not permit the disposal of assets subject to a foreign confiscation order where notified by the competent authority that such an order has been issued.
